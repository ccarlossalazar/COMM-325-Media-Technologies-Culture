Website Entry #1: Cinematic Realism
Website Entry #1: Cinematic Realism:

Early theories of film gave rise to what are described as “the realist tendencies” of the medium. What is meant by “cinematic realism”? How do various authors we have read either forward or critique the relationship of aesthetic realism and the moving image? Your response should describe what you think Cinematic Realism is, with specific reference to readings from the course. You should also offer your own views on the topic, including what properties you think make cinema more realistic – and if you think this can or should be a goal to pursue. Include in your discussion specific analysis of a clip that you choose to analyze, either advocating for or critiquing ideas of realism. This should be different from the clips we've discussed in class. Include visuals in your web entry! Film stills! Photographs of topics or people you discuss! Also try to use thumbnails rather the url links when possible. Include this post in your blog and submit the link here. This post should be in the neighborhood of 500-600 words though can certainly be longer.

Website Entry #2: Genre and Spectatorship
Website Entry #2: Genre and Spectatorship:

View in its entirety a film that seems to fit within a specific genre and/or provides a good sustained example of the "male gaze." (Though not relevant for most, I’ve included examples from the SMC Library to give you a sense). Then write a brief (500-600 words) analysis of the film connecting how it approaches issues of mise-en-scene with larger questions of genre or the male gaze. Your analysis should directly engage with at least Neale, Williams, or Mulvey. Bringing in multiple readings can be helpful. You may certainly include ideas from multiple authors, especially those quoted in articles or discussed in class (e.g., Altman, which would be a good choice). Be sure to analyze in specific detail at least one scene as it demonstrates your larger thesis about what genre is and how it is working in your chosen example. You can choose any film that you think will work, but are encouraged to choose with genre in mind, rather than force a genre argument onto a film you happen to have watched recently.

Website Entry #3: Genre and Video Production Technique
Website Entry #3: Genre and Video Production Technique:

For this blog post, you will create two short (about 1 minute or less) videos. Each video should be a “genre exercise,” which means you should pick a genre and tell a story that works within your understanding of that genre. Think in terms of the semantic/syntactic approach and/or body genres discussed in class. Both of your videos should be within the same genre. The difference is that one should be a continuity narrative with a range of at least three shots (e.g., long shot, medium shot, close-up, etc.,.) while the other short should be montage. In your post, in addition to embedding your videos, you should then explain what makes these genre works and continuity/montage in a few brief sentences with specific reference to these concepts as discussed in class. (The overall goal, then, is to show both in concept and practice that you know what genre, continuity, and montage are and how they work). These are not expected to be masterworks. The precision of the edits, for example, is far less important than your conceptualization and some attention to mise-en-scene and shot composition. The goal, again, is for you to both reflect upon and apply technique in range of conceptual and practice-based arenas. Your description of why your works fit into a specific genre and specific film technique (think in terms of writing an artist’s statement about your own work) is as important as the images themselves.
