Cinematic Realism

When thinking about “cinematic realism” it refers to the style of filmmaking that aims to portray real feelings and events through specific characters and settings. In other words, it means keeping film simple as a means to make it feel more real to the audience as opposed to using excessive elements that make the film feel more unnatural. In our class, we have looked at two very important individuals who played a key role in the development of cinematic realism. We examined the theories of Siegfried Kracauer and Andrew Bazin, who are regarded as two of the most influential film theorists that developed many key ideas about cinematic realism. Cinematic realism to me means how accurate something is when it comes to film. Oftentimes in film you might notice scenes that don’t feel as normal, whether it is what the characters do or perhaps an event is just captured incorrectly. However, there are also films that hit the nail on the head and do a great job of making you feel like you yourself are experiencing that moment. Some things that I think are essential for making a film more realistic are definitely the smaller details in an event or in other words consistency and continuity. This can be expressed through different shot selections like conversation shots in films when you get the perspective of all parties involved in a conversation and the different shots presented in a single scene. An example of this can be the scene we examined in class from the movie, “Social Network” and the use of different shots in the opening scene when the two characters are having a conversation and we can see both of their perspectives. A scene I decided to examine was from the movie, “The Meyerowitz Stories”, in which we see the characters having a conversation and there is an open shot where the scene is captured and then transitions to close up shots of everyone's point of view during the conversation. I believe this is the vision that both Kracauer and Bazin share when it comes to cinematic realism although they express it a little differently. Kracauer focused more on the artistic approach of film by, “outlining the inherent similarities between photography and cinema, pointing out that cinema can be considered to be simply the next step in the evolution of photography. So, it follows that like a photograph, “a film is realistic because it correctly reproduces that part of the real world to which it refers” (Kracauer 299). His ideas around cinematic realism revolved around using realistic representations in film to portray this realism. Similarly, Bazin focused, “on Renoir’s penchant for the “long-take, deep-focus” style (Bazin). Bazin is insistent that this is the most true-to-form style of cinematic realism, and this assertion is entirely coherent with Kracauer’s Cinematic Approach.” (Edwards, 3). Bazin focused on capturing film in a way that was natural and making it a more immersive experience for the audience. Personally, I agreed a lot with Gregory Currie’s view of cinematic realism and how he did not directly disagree with the previous two theorists but believed cinematic realism involves a number of different approaches that can be used to represent reality instead of simply capturing a direct reflection of the real world. Currie states that, “The ‘long-take, deep-focus’ style is actually no more realistic in a cognitive sense than are other styles – say those employing soft focus or disjunctive editing techniques. And this is because the ‘long-take, deep-focus’ style requires a much smaller aperture size to render images, meaning the human eye would in fact not be able to perceive these images in the same way as the camera does under the same conditions (Currie 55).
